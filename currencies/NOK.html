    The Norwegian Krone (NOK): Introduction

    The Norwegian Krone is Norway's official currency, regulated by the Norges Bank (The Central Bank of Norway). It is the 14th most traded currency in the world. In English, the term 'Krone' is known as 'Crown,' so the currency is also known as the Norwegian Crown.

    The Norwegian Krone is spent in coins and banknotes. The Krone coins have denominations of 1, 5, 10, and 20, while the Krone banknotes come in 50, 100, 200, 500, and 1000 denominations. Due to oil and gas exports, the currency is highly traded and has maintained a high value.

    History of Norwegian Krone

    The Norwegian Krone was introduced in 1875, replacing the Norwegian Speciedaler. The change in currency happened after Norway joined the Scandinavian Monetary Union, which was set up by Denmark and Sweden. The Krone had a fixed exchange rate with other member currencies during the union, which functioned with the gold standard. One Krone was equal to 0.403 grams of gold.

    When the union ended after World War I began in 1914, Krone continued with the gold standard. This continued until the Germans occupied Norway, which pegged the Norwegian Krone to the Reichsmark. After World War II, the Krone was pegged to the British Pound and later the US Dollar during the Bretton Woods system. Norway later adopted the floating exchange rate for the Krone in 1972. The value of Norwegian Krone has since been subject to the foreign exchange market.

    Major Countries That Use the Norwegian Krone

    The Norwegian Krone, although exclusive to the country of Norway, is used by other territories. The Norwegian government controls the territories, although they are overseas. They're called Dependent territories. 

    Some of these dependent territories are Svalbard, Queen Maud Land, and Bouvet Island. Bouvet Island is an uninhabited volcanic island near Norway. Although the official currency is the Norwegian Krone, it is rarely used.

    The Norwegian Krone is also used when making payments in countries like Finland and Sweden. These countries share a border with Norway. The currency is also used in Hirtshals and Frederikshavn, which are ports in Denmark.

    Online Gambling Regulations in Norway

    The Norwegian law does not differentiate between land-based gambling and online gambling. The Norwegian Gambling Scheme Act uses "gambling schemes" for all gambling activities. 

    Generally, the gambling legislation prohibits any form of gambling that is not licensed. The Act provides that the NGA will only issue licenses to certain organizations. These organizations must have humanitarian or social benefits where they are.

    While organizations may apply for licenses, there is a monopoly on commercial gambling. As such, no entity operates commercial gambling in Norway except the government. The only entities that can get the license are those with humanitarian and social benefits.

    The state-owned Norsk Tipping and Norsk Rikstoto hold the right to provide commercial gambling activities in Norway. The Norsk Tipping provides all forms of casino games, gambling, and sports betting. Meanwhile, the Norsk Rikstoto provides horse race betting. There is no legal liability for playing at offshore or foreign online casinos in Norway.

    The Norwegian Gaming Authority

    The Norwegian Gaming Authority is the official authority regulating gambling in Norway. No form of gambling activity can occur without this authority's license. It supervises all forms of gambling activity to ensure the games are free and fair. 

    The NGA also issues licenses to operators who meet the licensing requirements. However, apart from the state-run entities, only a few organizations receive the license. 

    It is the duty of the authority to promote responsible gambling. It ensures that casinos provide tools like Self-exclusion options to curb gambling habits. It sends out advertisements and makes public awareness of the dangers of gambling. This is in a bid to protect consumers. Apart from the tools, they ensure that casinos take safety measures like data protection.
